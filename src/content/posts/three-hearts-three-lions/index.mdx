## D&D Legacy

*Three Hearts and Three Lions* is one of the most directly influential works on Dungeons & Dragons ever written. Gary Gygax listed it first among Poul Anderson's recommended works for good reason.

- **The Paladin** — Holger Carlsen, the novel's hero, is essentially the template for the D&D paladin class: a champion of Law, supernaturally capable against the forces of Chaos, with a code of honour and a holy destiny.
- **The regenerating troll** — Anderson's trolls heal almost any wound unless burned or hit with acid. Gary Gygax lifted this mechanic directly into D&D, where it persists to this day.
- **Law vs. Chaos** — The central conflict of the novel maps directly onto the alignment axis Gygax and Arneson built into the game. Holger fights for Law against the forces of Chaos — the same cosmic struggle that underlies the original alignment system.
- **The dwarf Hugi** — A gruff, pragmatic companion whose personality became part of the DNA of D&D dwarves.

## My Thoughts

### A quick note on the audiobook itself
The narrator is 10/10. Bronson Pinchot performs different voices and accents for each character. The narration enhanced the experience for me.

I started the audiobook on my morning commute and at first I thought there was a foreword and notes about the author at the beginning of the book. I was immediately sucked into the storytelling and when the narrator announced chapter 2 I realized the story had begun and at this point I was locked in. The story starts firmly in reality during WW2 as we learn a little about the main character before he wakes up naked in a magical land.

I am immediately gripped by the imagery, and having never read this one, immediately recognize names and references from other media that I love. It becomes immediately clear how much of an influence this book has had on the things I love. Going into chapter 3 it feels strangely nostalgic, even though the book itself is new to me. Evocative tropes and I am forced to remind myself this was written before the tropes existed. At least in the traditional sense. Since the archetypes have likely always existed. What's true is, was, and always will be true.
